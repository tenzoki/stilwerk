# Example: Conversational Technical Writing

This is an excerpt in the style of Paul Graham's essays - conversational, opinionated, and concrete.

---

The thing I've noticed about good programmers is they're lazy. Not lazy in the sense of avoiding work, but lazy in the sense of hating unnecessary work. They automate things. They write tools. They refuse to do the same thing twice if there's any way around it.

I think this is actually the key insight. Most people think programming is about being smart, but that's only part of it. The real skill is being efficiently lazy. Why write the same boilerplate ten times when you can write a generator once?

This applies beyond code. When I see a company with elaborate processes for everything, I get suspicious. Good companies figure out what actually matters and ignore the rest. They don't have meetings about meetings.

Here's a concrete example. At YC, we used to have this elaborate application review process. Multiple rounds, scoring rubrics, the works. Then we realized something: we could predict which companies we'd fund after reading the application for about thirty seconds. So we simplified. We still read everything carefully, but we stopped pretending that elaborate processes made us smarter.

Does this mean we miss some good companies? Probably. But we definitely save time that we can spend actually helping the companies we do fund. It's a trade-off, and I think we're on the right side of it.

I'm not sure this advice generalizes, but it's worked for us.
