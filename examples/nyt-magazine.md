I recently visited Pallier, a watchmaker and former industrial designer, at the narrow workshop where he and Lena Förster run their repair business, Uhrglas. Pallier, a compact and wire-spectacled 44-year-old with the manner of a Swiss pharmacist, sat before a desk-mounted CNC micro-mill. On its platform, a titanium case back — destined for a client's vintage Longines — was being carved to the hundredth of a millimeter. The machine hummed and ticked; a fine mist of coolant drifted over the work surface. After a few passes, the spindle retracted, and the display read: "Cycle finished."

Pallier grew up in Lyon in the '80s, learning to work metal the old-fashioned way: He filed it down, stroke by patient stroke. After trade school, he held positions at workshops in Geneva for houses like Patek Philippe before becoming a restorer of four private collections. Back then, fabricating a replacement part meant spending days at the bench, squinting through a loupe, coaxing shape from raw stock with hand tools.

All that ended two years ago. Micro-machining had become so precise and so affordable that Pallier, initially skeptical, began delegating more and more to the mill. Now the CNC does the bulk of it. The cuts are so clean — and generally so faithful to spec — that when a collector recently needed Uhrglas to fabricate an obsolete crown tube, it took only forty minutes. In the before times? "That alone would have taken me two full days," he said. He and Förster, who is 37, are still watchmakers, but like a growing number of their peers now, they only rarely shape metal by hand. Instead, they spend their days talking to the software, drawing in CAD what they need and adjusting the machine's proposed tool paths. Then they press start.

Precision machining being precision machining, things occasionally go sideways. Sometimes when the mill chatters and gouges the surface, Pallier stops the cycle and re-clamps the stock: You really cannot skip the facing pass on this alloy.

To avoid repeating these sorts of errors, Pallier has added some firm constraints to his setup file, the list of parameters — a stern Ten Commandments — that the machine must observe before it begins any cut. When you behold the setup file of a craftsman using CNC, you are viewing a record of the maker's attempts to restrain the machine's generally reliable, but unpredictably willful, behavior.
